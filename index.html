Some Background

This is an exercise in data and story telling, produced while I was at the Connecticut Council for Education Reform. For this project, I downloaded and compiled data on 4th grade average educational outcomes in mathematics between students at the poverty level and those who are not, from 2003 to 2015, via the National Assessment on Educational Progress (NAEP). Eligibility for the national school lunch program is used as a proxy measure for a student's economic status. This was made possible by Ramnath Vaidyanathan and his work on rMaps. The images are Joseph Egan and Hunter Thomson productions, via Design Boom. The theme is from Start Bootstrap.

The Larger Issues Informing This Project

Poverty negatively impacts academic achievement the world over. [1] Results from the Teaching and Learning International Survey (TALIS) in 2012 --- conducted and published by the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) --- reveal growing economic disparity among U.S. students. [2] About one in five children in the U.S. live below the federal poverty level, and poverty rates appear to be rising. [3, 4]

The Data

Below is a map visualizing the difference in average scale scores in NAEP mathematics between 4th grade students not eligible for school lunches (non-poverty) and those who are (poverty). The darker the state, the worse the achievement gap. Sliding the scale to see how the differences change per state over the years from 2003 to 2015, while the range of the achievement gap has decreased nationwide, it has generally worsened throughout the country.

Achievement Gap Between Non-Poverty Students and Their Peers: Unranked




This next map ranks these differences in average scale scores in total (over 2003 to 2015), and colors each state according to decile. The darker the state, the worse the achievement gap, relative to other states. Connecticut, Illinois, and Maryland have consistently had the worst achievement gap between poverty and non-poverty students over the years. The achievement gap has worsened in California, Colorado, Minnesota, New York, Ohio, Texas, and Washington.

Achievement Gap Between Non-Poverty Students and Their Peers: Ranked




A couple things to note: Correlation is not causation; and educational outcomes are dependent on many factors, not just what happens in the classroom.



However, it's clear that the U.S. education system and the socioeconomic policies supporting it need to evolve.
